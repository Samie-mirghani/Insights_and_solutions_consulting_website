The Last Eggs in Western Sudan

Community Systems Support January 28, 2026

Community support systems serve as a lifeline in many countries and the foundation for community development. This is particularly true in Sudan.

Early in my career, I served as a regional manager in El Obeid in western Sudan for an international organization where I made an unexpected connection with a young girl and her family that shaped my view of community development.

I was on the road almost constantly, traveling from one project site or office to another. I found it difficult to sit still in my office for long. I still do.

Looking back, my time in western Sudan served as a profound political, technical, and management learning experience. I was in charge of a large staff, two sub-offices, warehouses full of humanitarian supplies, and a programmatic territory similar in size to California and Nevada combined.

One site visit in particular stands out. Inside a mud and thatch hut, a young girl slowly approached me, stopping each time to seek her mother’s approval. For a brief moment, she placed her hand in mine before running back to her family.

As we prepared to leave, the family offered us two eggs. Refusing would have been a serious insult. In western Sudan, travelers are always given food and shelter — rooted in the belief that tomorrow it may be you or a hungry family member on the road.

Today, these traditional Sudanese systems of mutual protection are keeping communities alive amid conflict and hardship. Neighbors still share food, medicine, and care — including, no doubt, the last eggs in many households.
